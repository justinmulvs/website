Essentialism

Essentialism, while not a deeply philosophical read, is certainly a reaction to the problems of or time. In an age where people to struggle to find meaning in a world of "busy", Essentialism is a philosophy to escape the bullshit.


Essence


1. The Essentialist
  1. Value Proposition: Once you give yourself permission to stop trying to do it all, to stop saying yes to everyone, you can make your highest contribution toward the things that really matter.
  2. Less but Better. Pursue this in a disciplined way.
  3. Am I investing in the right activities? Making the wisest possible investment of your time and energy. If you don’t prioritize your life, someone else will.
  4. Success arises from Clarity of purpose, but then tugs away from it through distraction.
  5. Modern day issues: Overabundance of choice bringing about decision fatigue, social pressure/connectedness to do more, the idea that you can/have to do it all now, 
  6. Methods for elimination:
    1. Explore and evaluate: Do I love this?, Do I do/use it often?, Does this make the highest contribution to my goal?
    2. Eliminate: If I did not already own this/wasn’t pre-committed, how much would I pay/would I do this?
    3. Execute: Need a systematic method to ensure it gets done.
  7. There is a huge tradeoff between a lot of good things and a few great. 
  8. Core-Mindset of an essentialist:
    1. Individual Choice: I can choose how to spend my energy and time.
    2. The prevalence of noise: Almost everything is noise, and a very few things are exceptionally valuable.
    3.  The Reality of tradeoffs: We can’t have or do it all.
  9. Essentialist explore everything. Seemingly paradoxical, you do so to evaluate options before committing.
  10. Highest contribution: What am I inspired by? What am I talented at? What meets a significant need in the world? Looking to do the right thing, in the right way, at the right time.

2. Choose: The Invincible Power of Choice
  1. Choice is not a thing, it is an action. The ability to choose cannot be taken away, it can only be forgotten.
  2.  Replace “I have to” with “I choose to”. “My first act of free will shall be to believe in free will”.

3. Discern: The Unimportance of Practically Everything
  1. Certain types of effort yield higher results.
  2. Pareto Principal: Focusing on building/improving key features will yield best results. 20% of your efforts drive 80% of your results.
  3. There are exponential differences in results for certain things. 10,000X difference (explains tremendous success vs. nobody).
  4. Essentialists think almost everything is non-essential. They distinguish the vital few from the trivial many.

4. Trade-off: Which Problem Do I want?
  1. Straddling: Keeping your current strategy intact while simultaneously trying to adopt another.
  2. Trade-offs are real. We cannot have it all.
  3. Essentialists don’t ask "How Can I Do Both"? They ask
    "Which problem do I want"? 
  4. It is not “What do I have to Give Up?” but “What do I want go Big on?”. Trade-offs are just part of life.



Evaluate

5. Escape: The Perks of Being Unavailable
  1. Don’t get so busy doing things that you can’t even determine if they’re worthwhile.
  2. Create space to explore life.
  3. Focus is something we do. Like eyesight, focus is not on one thing, but adjusting and adapting to our field of vision.
  4. The busier we are, the more we need to take time to think and meditate.
  5. Bill Gates takes two weeks off to read and think  at the busiest parts of the year 

6. Look: See What Really Matters
  1. Take the Who, What, Where, When, How, and Why, and answer What is the point?
  2. Pay attention to the signal in the noise. Hear what is not being said. Scan to find the essence of the information.
  3. Keep a journal. Review entries every 90 days or so.
  4. Get out into the field. Talk to people. Find the essence of what matters.
  5. Look for unusual/abnormal details.
  6. Clarify the question.

7. Play: Embrace the Wisdom of Your Inner Child
  1. School is derived from greek “skole” meaning leisure.
  2. Play is essential and sparks creativity and innovation. 
  3. Play broadens perspectives and adds options. It cures stress. It stimulates both organized and creative parts of the brain. So go play.

8. Sleep: Protect the Asset
  1. Protect your mind, body, and spirit. Pace yourself, nurture yourself, and give yourself fuel to thrive.
  2. Sleep. is a priority, breeds creativity, is for high performers, enables productivity and high level of mental contribution.
  3. A nap can help with creativity and decision making.

9. Select: The Power of Extreme Criteria
  1. The answer is either “HELL YEA” or “No”.
  2. Rate choices from 0 to 100. Drop anything below a 90. Shifts to logical evaluation over emotional.
  3. Make decisions by design, not default.Use narrow criteria like “Is this exactly what I’m looking for?"
  4. Method for selection: Write down the opportunity being offered. Write down your three minimum criteria for accepting. Write down three ideal criteria for accepting. The opportunity must possess all of the the first three and two of the second.


Eliminate
If I didn’t have this opportunity, what would I be willing to do to acquire it?

10. Clarify: One Decision that Makes a Thousand
  1. Clarity of purpose consistently predicts success. Lack of clarity creates confusion, stress, and frustration. 
  2. Lack of clarity leads to playing politics and teams functioning poorly. 
  3. Pursuing multiple different paths well does not necessarily sum to a meaningful whole. 
  4. Mission: General and inspirational. Shoot for Essential Intent: concrete and general. Meaningful and measurable
  5. Make one decision that eliminates one thousand
  6. Crafting: ask "if we could truly be excellent at only one thing, what would it be?" and "how will we know when we're done?" 

11. Dare: The power of a graceful no
  1. A well done no can change the world— Rosa parks. 
  2. Lack of clarity makes it unclear what we should say yes or no to. 
  3. We say yes to avoid social conflict. 
  4. How to say no: separate decisions from relationships, choose no but don't say it (I'm flattered, but I'm afraid I'm overcommitted). Focus on the trade-off. Be aware everyone is selling something.  No involves trading popularity for respect. 
  5. A clear no can be more graceful than a vague/noncommittal yes. 
  6. Tactics for saying no:
    1. take a three second pause to consider (the awkward pause)
    2. say it politely/delay (busy now, but maybe when I'm freed up)
    3. default to "let me check my calendar"
    4. have an e-mail auto responder when busy
    5. inform a superior of how it would disrupt other work
    6. use humor
    7. clearly state "you're welcome to do this, but I will do this" as a means of outlining what you won't do.
    8. I can't do it but X might be able to

12. Uncommit: Win Big By Cutting Your Losses
  1. Avoid Sunk-Cost Bias. 
  2. Essentials are comfortable with cutting losses. Ask “If I weren’t already invested, how much would I invest now?” and “What else could I do with this time and money?"
  3. When you own a project/item, it is harder to let go. Fixes:
    1. Ask, if I didn’t own it, how much would I pay? If I didn’t already have this obligation, would I take it?
    2. Let go of the fear of waste.
    3. Admit failure to begin success.
    4. Get a neutral second opinion.
    5. Avoid Status Quo bias: The tendency to continue doing something just because we always have done it.
  4. Use Zero-Based budgeting: Don’t budget time/energy based on what you’ve previously been doing, start from scratch and justify everything.
  5. Run a Reverse Pilot: Ask if anything negative will arise from letting go.

13. Edit: The Invisible Art
  1. Good editors make it hard not to see what’s important.
  2. Use Deliberate Subtraction to add life and energy.
  3. Eliminated distraction to add value.
  4. How:
    1. Cut-out options. Not activities, options for activities. There are things you just don’t do.
    2. Condense: Lower the ratio of effort to results. Replace the meaningless with meaningful.
    3. Correct: Relate all activities back to your core cause.
    4. Edit Less: Edit less. get involved less. Allow more to happen before we jump in.

14. The Freedom of Setting Boundaries
  1. Boundaries proactively eliminate the demands and encumbrances from others that distract us. 
  2. Essentialists: know that which limits you to become limitless. See boundaries as liberating. Set rules that eliminate the need for no. 
  3. Help people find solutions, but don't rob them of their problems. Build fences ahead of time. Think fence around a playground. Clear boundaries allow for us to explore all options within them.
  4. Know your dealbreakers. Write down every time you feel a boundary is crossed. This helps delineate your personal boundaries. Craft social contracts. 


Execute.
Doing the essential things. 

15. buffer: the unfair advantage
  1. Buffer: something which prevents two things from coming in contact and harming each other. 
  2. Build buffers. Practice extreme and early preparation. 
  3. Use good times to creat a buffer for the bad. How?
  4. Prepare for the worst. Add 50% to your time estimate. People admit having a tendency to underestimate while believing current estimates are correct. 
  5. Ask: what risks do you face? What is the worst-case scenario? What are the social implications? Financial? How can you invest to reduce risks. 

16. Subtract: bring forth more by removing obstacles
  1. Find the biggest thing slowing you down and alleviate it. Get better/lighten the load. 
  2. Be clear on your essential intent. Can't know what to remove until we know what we want. 
  3. Identify the slowest hiker. What obstacle, if removed, would make most others disappear? If possible, remove the obstacle. 

17. Progress: The power of small wins
  1. Essentialists: Starts small and get big results. Celebrates small acts of progress.
  2. Minimum Viable Progress: What is the smallest bit I can do that will be valuable to the task?
  3. Minimum Viable Preparation: Start preparing far ahead of time by jotting down ideas, establishing outlines/frameworks, or dictating key points.
  4. Visualize your progress.

18. Flow: The Genius of Routine
  1. Make essential the default position. Create a routine that enshrines the essential, making execution effortless.
  2. To change bad habits: Identify cue’s, associate with something essential.
  3. Create new triggers: Develop a cue to indicate you should do something. Every time you do the habit gets stronger.
  4. Focus on the hardest thing first. 
  5. Mix up your routines: Different routines for different days.

19. Focus: What’s important now?
  1. Stay focused on yourself in the now. There is a difference between losing and being beaten.
  2. Essentialists: Live in the moment, focus on what is important now, enjoy the present
  3. We can only focus on one thing at a time. Don’t multitask. When people are on their phones and you’re speaking, pause until they’re done.
  4.  How to be present:
    1. When feeling stressed, stop, breathe, and take time what needs to be done right now.
    2. Write down all the ideas buzzing around in your head.

20. Be: The Essential Life
  1. Focus on less achievements, but better, bigger. More powerful.
  2. Focus on opportunities which bring you clarity. Ignore others which contribute to the haze.
  3. Gives you the opportunity to experience more joy, to live life more fully.
  4. Whenever making a decision ask: What is essential? In the end, everything else fades away.
  5. Clarity=Success
